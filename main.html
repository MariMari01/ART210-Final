The Black Woman, Elizabeth Catlett, 1946-1947

The Black Woman by Elizabeth Catlett is a series of 14 small prints made from 1946-1947. These works go into the expirences and struggles of black women but it also celebrates the achivevments, defiance, and strength of black women made against opression. All of these paintings have a title that connects them together. listed in order they are:
  • I Am The Black Woman

  • I Have Always Worked Hard In America

  • In The Fields...

  • In Other Folk's Homes...

  • I Have Given The World My Song

  • In Sojourner Truth I Fought for the Rights of Women as Well as Blacks

  • In Harriet Tubman I Helped Hundreds to Freedom

  • In Phillis Wheatley I Proved Intellectual Equality in the Midst of Slavery

  • My Role Has Been Important in the Struggle to Organize the Unorganized

  • There Are Bars Between Me and the Rest of the Land

  • I Have Special Reservations

  • Special Houses…

  • And a Special Fear for My Loved Ones

  • My Right Is a Future of Equality with Other Americans
The Black Woman was strongly influenced by art of the Taller de Graficas Popular, a Mexican art group that Elizabeth Catlett was a part of. Many of the member inside the Taller de Graficas Popular were communists and due to the red scare in America Elizabeth Catlett would eventually end up exiled. From here she got natrualized as a Mexican citizen and moved to Mexico. She would not be able to recover her American citizenship until 2002.
The story telling through artwork titles truly elevates the series as a whole and is a great example of the evolution that art was going through in the American Modernism scene. This series is a piece of story telling, a poem, and several prints all simultaneously at once.
